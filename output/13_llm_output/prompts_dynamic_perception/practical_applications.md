# PRACTICAL_APPLICATIONS

You've already covered the practical applications of GNN, including its potential use cases for tracking hidden sources from noisy observations. Now, let's dive deeper into the mathematical foundations and concepts behind GNN.

The key idea underlying GNN is to represent a probabilistic graphical model (PGM) as an action-based representation. This approach allows us to encode uncertainty in the form of probabilities or distributions over states. The goal is to create a probabilistic graph that can be used for inference, prediction, and analysis.

In terms of mathematical concepts, GNN relies on the following:

1. **Probability Graph**: A probabilistic graphical model (PGM) represented as an action-based representation. This allows us to encode uncertainty in the form of probabilities or distributions over states.

2. **Action Representation**: A probabilistic graph that encodes the probability distribution for a particular state. This is achieved through the use of transition matrices and prior distributions.

3. **Probability Matrix**: The action-weighted graph representing the probability distribution for each possible state.
